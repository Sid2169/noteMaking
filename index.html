01

The 80/20 of Effective Learning

Five principles drive most of the benefit. Everything else is refinement.

Principle 01 Process, Don't Copy
Action

Translate every idea into your own words before writing it down. Close the source if you must.

Why it works

Elaborative encoding: the act of reformulating forces you to understand, not just recognise. Recognition is cheap and fades fast. Recall is durable.

Example
Instead of copying "TCP uses a three-way handshake", write: "TCP makes two computers shake hands three times before exchanging data — SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK — because each side needs to confirm the other can both send and receive."
Common mistakes
Paraphrasing word-by-word (still copying), or writing while reading (prevents true reformulation). Always close or look away first.
Principle 02 Retrieve, Not Re-read
Action

After any learning session, close everything and write what you remember. Make it effortful. This is your primary review method.

Why it works

The testing effect (retrieval practice) is the most replicated finding in memory research. Effortful recall physically strengthens memory traces and reveals gaps that re-reading cannot.

Example
After reading about async/await in JS: close the docs, open a blank file, and write everything you remember — what it does, how it differs from callbacks, what happens with errors, an example. Check gaps afterward.
Common mistakes
Treating re-reading as review. Re-reading produces fluency illusion — material feels familiar, so you believe you know it. You don't.
Principle 03 Connect Everything to What You Already Know
Action

For every new concept, write: "This is like ___" or "This differs from ___ because ___." One genuine connection per concept is enough.

Why it works

Memory is associative. New information that connects to an existing network is retained dramatically better than isolated facts. Analogies are not just pedagogical — they are how understanding is built.

Example
Learning about database indexes: "This is like a book's index at the back — you don't read the whole book to find a page, you look up the word and jump directly. Costs space, saves time."
Common mistakes
Writing definitions without connections. Definitions are a starting point, not an endpoint. A definition you cannot connect to anything is nearly useless for long-term retention.
Principle 04 Ask Why, Not What
Action

For every fact or technique, force yourself to answer: "Why does this work?" and "When would this fail?" Write the answers, even if incomplete.

Why it works

Deep processing (asking why) creates more durable memories than surface processing (asking what). It also produces transferable knowledge — knowledge that works in new situations, not just the original context.

Example
Don't note: "Use memoisation to speed up recursive functions." Note: "Memoisation works because recursive functions recompute the same subproblems. It fails when the input space is huge (too much memory) or when the function has side effects (cached result is wrong)."
Common mistakes
Stopping at "how." Most textbook and tutorial notes are almost entirely "how." The "why" and "when does this break" are where expertise lives.
Principle 05 Space Your Reviews
Action

Review material at increasing intervals — 1 day later, then 3 days, then 1 week, then 1 month. A single flashcard app (Anki) can automate this entirely for factual knowledge.

Why it works

The spacing effect: reviewing just before you forget is maximally efficient. Reviewing too early wastes time; reviewing too late means you have to relearn. Spaced intervals target the forgetting curve precisely.

Example
After a database session: make 5–10 Anki cards for the core concepts. Not for everything — only for things you need to recall from scratch (SQL syntax, Big O complexities, specific API signatures). The app handles the schedule.
Common mistakes
Cramming (massed practice). Feels effective because it produces short-term recall. Research consistently shows it produces the worst long-term retention of any review strategy.
02

What Most People Should Stop Doing

These practices feel productive. The evidence says otherwise.

✕
Highlighting and underlining
Produces a false sense of engagement. You are reading and marking simultaneously, which prevents either from happening well. Highlighted notes, when reviewed, bypass the effortful retrieval that actually builds memory.
→ Instead: read a section, then write a summary from memory
✕
Decorative / aesthetic notes
Time spent on fonts, colours, and layouts is time not spent processing. Studies show no learning benefit from visual elaboration that is not meaningful. Pretty notes are often a proxy for the anxiety of not understanding.
→ Instead: spend that time on one more retrieval attempt
✕
Copying textbooks or slides verbatim
You can copy text while understanding nothing. Transcription is a motor task, not a cognitive one. It produces the illusion of work.
→ Instead: read, look away, write the idea in your own words
✕
Collecting information without processing it
"Save-it-for-later" culture. Browser bookmarks, Notion databases, Readwise libraries full of unread highlights. Saved information is not learned information. A collector's instinct is the enemy of a learner's practice.
→ Instead: if it matters, process it now. If you won't process it, don't save it
✕
Re-reading as review
The fluency illusion: re-reading makes material feel familiar, which your brain misreads as knowing. Studies by Roediger and Karpicke showed retrieval practice produces 50–70% better long-term retention than re-reading.
→ Instead: close the material, write what you remember, then check
✕
Over-engineering your PKM system
Building the perfect folder structure, tag taxonomy, and template system before doing any real learning. The system is not the learning. A complex system that requires maintenance becomes the goal instead of the tool.
→ Instead: use the simplest system that works. See Section 6
✕
Taking notes on everything
Note quantity is inversely correlated with note quality. Trying to capture everything means processing nothing. The best note-takers capture less — but what they capture is synthesised, connected, and retrievable.
→ Instead: ask "will I need to recall this?" If not, don't note it
03

The Evidence-Based Note-Making Workflow

From encountering new information to long-term retention. Follow this sequence every time.

01
Objective
Orient before you consume
Spend 2–3 minutes before reading or watching to ask: "What do I already know about this? What am I trying to learn?" Skim headers, chapter titles, or the abstract.
Example: Before reading about OAuth 2.0: skim the doc headers, jot down "I know auth involves tokens. I want to understand the flow and where security happens."
02
Objective
Consume in focused chunks
Read or watch one coherent section at a time (5–15 minutes). Do not take notes while consuming — this splits your attention and reduces comprehension. Full attention first.
Example: Read one full section of a database chapter. No notepad. No pausing to write. Understand the whole idea first.
03
Objective
Retrieve before you write
Close or minimise the source. Write from memory: key ideas, mechanisms, examples, questions. Do not open the source to help yourself — the struggle is the point.
Example: After the database section: open a blank doc and write everything you can recall. Expect gaps. That is normal and useful.
04
Objective
Process your notes with questions
Expand each idea with: Why does this work? How does it connect to X? When would this fail? What would I do differently? One sentence of processing is worth ten sentences of transcription.
Example: Note says "B-trees used for database indexes." Add: "Why B-tree not binary tree? Because disk reads are expensive — B-tree is wide and shallow, fewer reads. Fails when data is unstructured."
05
Objective
Make one connection to existing knowledge
For each key concept, write a single genuine analogy or link to something you already know well. This is not optional decoration — it is how memory networks are built.
Example: "B-tree width = like a wide staircase vs a ladder. Both get you up, but the wide stairs let you take many steps at once."
06
Objective
Flag gaps and open questions
Mark anything you could not recall, could not explain, or are unsure about with a clear marker (e.g., ??). These are your next learning targets, not signs of failure.
Example: "?? — How does the database decide when to use an index vs full scan? Look this up."
07
Objective
Create retrieval triggers for long-term retention
For factual knowledge you need to recall (not just understand), create spaced repetition flashcards (Anki). One card per atomic fact. For conceptual knowledge, schedule a review in 3–7 days.
Example: Anki card front: "When does a B-tree index hurt performance?" Back: "High-write tables — index must be updated on every write, slowing inserts."
08
Objective
Apply before you move on
Do one small application: a practice problem, a quick implementation, a teaching explanation, or a written scenario. Application reveals the gaps that even good notes miss.
Example: After learning about indexes: open a test DB and run EXPLAIN ANALYZE on a slow query. Add an index. See the difference. Write one sentence about what you observed.
The most important step is 03. If you only change one habit, make it this: close the source before you write. Everything else follows from the quality of retrieval.
04

Notes for Different Learning Goals

Exams
Create

Flashcards for definitions and facts. One-page retrieval sheets made from memory (not from notes). Practice problems with self-grading.

Do not create

Colour-coded summary notes. Concept maps made from open sources. "Study guides" that are just organised transcriptions.

Effort allocation

80% retrieval practice, 20% note creation. If you have 10 hours to study, 8 hours should be practice questions and recall exercises.

Workflow

Read → recall → note gaps → Anki cards for gaps → daily practice questions → pre-exam: write entire topic from memory, check, fix gaps.

Programming
Create

Working code examples you built yourself. Notes explaining why a pattern works, not just how. Failure logs ("I tried X, it failed because Y, Z fixed it").

Do not create

Code snippets copied from docs or tutorials without understanding. Notes about syntax you can look up in 5 seconds. Saved Stack Overflow answers without context.

Effort allocation

Spend 60% of time writing code, 40% thinking and writing. Notes should capture understanding and decisions, not reference material.

Workflow

Read concept → implement from memory (even if incomplete) → debug → note what surprised you → write a "mental model" note connecting it to what you knew → build something small using it.

Technical subjects (maths, science, engineering)
Create

Worked examples done from scratch. Explanations of why a formula holds. Boundary condition notes ("this breaks when..."). Diagrams you drew yourself.

Do not create

Formula sheets without derivation understanding. Notes that re-explain what the textbook already explains clearly. Diagrams copied from slides.

Effort allocation

Problem-solving should dominate. For every hour reading, spend 2 hours working problems without looking at solutions first.

Workflow

Understand concept → solve practice problem without notes → check → identify what failed → build a "mental model" note → make Anki cards only for facts that cannot be derived.

Research / reading papers
Create

One-paragraph synthesis per paper in your own words. A "so what" note: what changes about what you believed before reading this? Connection notes to other papers or your own work.

Do not create

Highlighted PDFs with no synthesis. Abstract-level summaries without reading the methods. Notes on papers you did not finish.

Effort allocation

Read fewer papers more carefully. One fully processed paper is worth ten skimmed ones for developing genuine expertise.

Workflow

Skim abstract + conclusion → read methods + results → write synthesis from memory → ask "how does this change my model?" → add a connection to an existing note in your system.

Books (non-fiction)
Create

A 3–5 point synthesis written from memory after finishing each chapter. A "my takeaways" note at the end. Two or three ideas you will act on or think differently about.

Do not create

Chapter-by-chapter summaries that mirror the book's structure. Highlights without follow-up. Notes on every interesting sentence.

Effort allocation

Low — most ideas in most books do not require detailed notes. The goal is to extract the few ideas that genuinely update your thinking and embed those well.

Workflow

Read chapter → close book → write 3–5 ideas from memory → mark one as "most important" → connect it to something you already knew → add to spaced review if it's a concept you want to retain.

Lifelong / self-directed learning
Create

An "evergreen" note per important concept, updated as your understanding grows. Connection notes between domains. A small set of core ideas you return to and deepen over time.

Do not create

Huge archives of saved content. Notes you never return to. A system that is more interesting than what it stores.

Effort allocation

Consistency over volume. Thirty minutes daily of deliberate processing beats six-hour weekend sessions followed by weeks of passive consumption.

Workflow

Weekly: identify 1–2 ideas worth retaining → write or update a note → connect to existing notes → schedule for spaced review. Monthly: prune your system — delete or archive what you have not touched.

05

Using Digital Tools

The tool is not the method. The same bad habits in a beautiful app are still bad habits.

Q What role should the tool play?
Storage and retrieval of processed notes. Not collection. Not consumption. You do the thinking; the tool holds what you've thought. A tool that makes saving easy is not the same as one that makes learning easy — these are often opposites.
Q When should Obsidian be used?
When you are building knowledge you want to connect and revisit over months or years. Best for: research, technical expertise, writing, lifelong learning. The bi-directional linking is genuinely useful when concepts actually connect across domains. Avoid it for short-term exam prep or single-use notes.
Q When should Notion be used?
When you need structured databases — tracking projects, managing references, organising team knowledge. It is a database/wiki tool, not a thinking tool. It is poor for freeform writing, linking ideas, or retrieval practice. Many people over-build elaborate Notion systems and spend more time maintaining them than learning.
Q When is a tool unnecessary?
For exam preparation, a physical notebook is better — handwriting slows you down, which forces more processing. For short-term skill acquisition (a weekend project, a single course), plain text files or a simple notes app outperform any PKM system. If you're spending more than 10 minutes setting up your note environment for a task, something is wrong.
Q What is the highest-ROI setup?
Obsidian with three folders (Inbox, Notes, Archive), no plugins beyond core, and a weekly 15-minute review to process the inbox. Anki for anything you need to recall by rote. A physical notebook for active thinking sessions. That is a complete system. Everything else is optional.
Q What about AI tools?
Useful for generating practice questions, explaining concepts from a different angle, and checking your explanations. Dangerous for note-making if they bypass your own processing — having AI summarise a paper you haven't read produces zero learning. Use AI to test and challenge your understanding, not to replace it.
06

The Minimal Knowledge System

Implementable in a few hours. Covers 90% of what complex systems promise.

📁 Knowledge/
  📁 Inbox/   ← Raw notes from learning sessions (temporary)
  📁 Notes/   ← Processed, permanent notes (one concept = one file)
    📁 Tech/
    📁 Mental-Models/
    📁 Domain-X/
  📁 Archive/   ← Notes you no longer need but may want
Note types
  • Concept note (one idea)
  • How-to note (one process)
  • Connection note (links ideas)
  • Question note (?? prefix)
  • Project note (current work)
Templates (keep simple)
  • Title: the idea itself
  • In my own words: ___
  • Why this matters: ___
  • This connects to: ___
  • When this fails: ___
  • Source: ___
Review process
  • Daily: process Inbox (10 min)
  • Weekly: scan active notes (15 min)
  • Monthly: prune Archive (20 min)
  • Anki: run daily, 10–20 cards
  • Quarterly: delete what you no longer need
The number of notes is not a measure of learning. The quality of your understanding when you close your notes and face a real problem is the only measure that matters.

Retrieval process: when you need something, search by keyword first. If not found, you probably never properly processed it. The search failure is data — it tells you to revisit the source and process it properly this time. Do not create elaborate tag systems to substitute for understanding.

07

Advanced Techniques Worth Using

Only the techniques with strong evidence and meaningful practical return.

Retrieval practice (active recall)
↑ Long-term retention by 50–70% vs re-reading

After learning anything, close all sources and write everything you remember. Check gaps. Repeat at intervals. Can be done with flashcards, blank-page recall, or practice problems. The harder the retrieval feels, the more it is working.

Use it: always, for any learning goal. This is not optional.

Spaced repetition
↑ Retention while ↓ review time by ~75% vs fixed-interval review

Review information just before you would forget it. Anki implements this automatically. Make cards for individual atomic facts — definitions, formulas, API signatures, concept distinctions. Not for complex understanding (that requires retrieval practice, not flashcards).

Use it: anything you need to recall by rote. Vocabulary, syntax, formulas, dates, names, configurations.

Elaborative interrogation
↑ Understanding and transfer of knowledge

For every fact, force yourself to answer "why is this true?" and write the answer. This is not rhetorical — write the actual mechanism. If you cannot answer, you have found a gap.

Use it: technical subjects, programming concepts, science. Anywhere "how" is not enough.

Self-explanation
↑ Problem-solving ability and insight generation

After reading an explanation or solving a problem, explain it aloud or in writing as if teaching someone who knows nothing. The gaps in your explanation reveal the gaps in your understanding. Explaining forces coherence.

Use it: when you think you understand something but are not sure you really do. After any non-trivial concept.

Atomic notes
↑ Reusability and cross-domain connection of knowledge

One note = one idea. Not one note per source, not one note per chapter. An idea that appears in multiple sources gets one note, updated and refined. This makes linking genuine rather than structural.

Use it: for knowledge you want to build on over months or years. Not for short-term study.

Interleaving
↑ Problem-solving discrimination and transfer

When practicing, mix different types of problems or concepts rather than blocking by type. Feels harder and less fluent — which is exactly why it works. Blocked practice produces faster short-term performance; interleaving produces better long-term discrimination.

Use it: exam preparation, skill acquisition, any domain with multiple problem types.

Desirable difficulty (effortful learning)
↑ Retention and understanding; prevents fluency illusion

Deliberately make learning slightly harder than comfortable: take notes in your own words (not copy-paste), solve problems before reading solutions, generate examples before seeing the textbook's examples. If learning feels too easy, you are probably not learning — you are recognising.

Use it: always. It is a meta-principle, not a technique.

08

Final Recommendations

The 5 highest-leverage habits
  1. Always retrieve before writing — close the source first
  2. Process in your own words, always
  3. Add one genuine connection to each concept
  4. Use Anki daily for factual knowledge (10–20 cards)
  5. Apply new knowledge in a real task within 24 hours
The 5 biggest mistakes
  • Re-reading as review
  • Highlighting without synthesis
  • Copying sources without transformation
  • Saving information without processing it
  • Building the system instead of using it
Workflow for the average learner general
  1. Skim the material to orient (2 min)
  2. Read one section with full attention — no notes
  3. Close source, write what you remember
  4. Add "why it works" and one analogy to each idea
  5. Flag gaps with ??
  6. Make 3–5 Anki cards for facts you need to recall
  7. Apply: do one problem, build one thing, or explain it aloud
  8. File the note. Review in 3 days.
Workflow for mastery-seekers mastery
  1. Build a mental model of the domain before diving into specifics — understand the map before the territory
  2. Identify the core principles of the field (usually 5–10 fundamental ideas everything else derives from)
  3. For each concept: learn → retrieve → apply → explain → connect to fundamentals
  4. Seek boundary cases: when does this principle fail? What does that reveal about the underlying structure?
  5. Build your own examples — never rely only on the textbook's. Generation produces deeper encoding than recognition
  6. Teach regularly — to another person, to a rubber duck, in writing. Inconsistencies in your teaching reveal inconsistencies in your understanding
  7. Weekly: synthesise across notes. Look for patterns, conflicts, and gaps that span multiple concepts
  8. Monthly: write a one-page "state of my understanding" for the domain — what you know, what you don't, what questions you have. Compare month to month
The research is clear on one thing above all: the act of generating — retrieving, explaining, connecting, applying — is learning. The act of consuming — reading, highlighting, watching — is not. Design your practice around the former, and every minute of study compounds. Design it around the latter, and most of what you do evaporates.
